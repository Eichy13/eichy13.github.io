About Skiing

Skiing is a popular winter sport and recreational activity that involves sliding down snow-covered slopes using skis attached to the feet. It is primarily practiced in mountainous regions with sufficient snowfall during the winter months. Skis are long, narrow, and curved at both ends to help with maneuverability and stability. The main objective of skiing is to navigate down the slopes while maintaining balance and control over the skis. Skiers typically use ski poles to assist with propulsion and balance. The sport can be enjoyed at various skill levels, from beginners on gentle slopes to expert skiers tackling challenging terrains and slopes.

For those who are learning to ski for the first time, there are basic techniques that need to be learnt to avoid hurting one self. One of these techniquesis is Wedge (Triangle) Skiing: This is a beginner-level skiing technique used to control speed and make basic turns.

Heres how to do it:

Step 1: Begin with your skis in a "V" shape, with the tips closer together and the tails wider apart, forming a wedge. Bend your knees slightly and keep your weight centered. This will help keep speed while sking down the mountain constant.

Step 2: To slow down, push the tails of the skis outward, increasing the wedge angle. The wider the wedge, the greater the friction, resulting in decreased speed. To go faster, push the tails of the skis inward, decreasing the wedge angle. The narrower the wedge, the lesser the friction, resulting in greater speed.

Step 3: To turn left, apply more pressure to the inside edge of your right ski, which will steer you to the left. To turn right, apply more pressure to the inside edge of your left ski, steering you to the right.

Step 4: As you complete the turn, gradually bring your skis back to a parallel position and prepare for the next turn. Then you repeat again.

Skills Guide

Usually when going to a ski resort, you may consider investing in a coach/guide to help teach/guide you around the mountain. Most school follow a level scale to help dictate what level of skill you are before pairing you with the right coach. This table will help give a rough idea on how the levels are usually distrubuted.

Skill Level My Current Ability What I want to learn Terrain
Level 1 (Beginner) I have never skied before. I cannot stop or glide Confidently stop and make single turns Flat green terrain, which may be accessed by a moving carpet
Level 2 (Started Learning to Turn) I can confidently stop in a snow plough on flat slopes (Green slopes) and can make single turns in a Snowplough (Triangle technique) Linking my turns together confidently to control speed I am comfortable on flat green terrain and may venture onto beginner terrain accessed by lifts
Level 3 (Able to Link Turns) I can confidently link turns and control my speed while in a Snowplough I am learning to match my skis earlier in my turn, and vary my turn size and shape (Starting to learn parallel turning) I am comfortable on green runs accessed by lifts. And would like to explore more similar terrain
Level 4 (Refining Turns) I can make parallel turns and a variety of turn sizes Looking to refine my parallel turns, including learning to pole plant. While exploring more varied terrain and snow conditions I can ski most red terrain, however more black runs are still a challenge
Level 5 (Intermediate) Strong parallel turns with a pole plant on all red terrain and some black terrain Learning how to manage a variety of snow conditions. Which includes riding powder, short turns, carving,moguls and off piste skiing Able to ski comfortably on all red and easy black runs. Learning to manage off piste terrain. May still struggle on some harder black runs
Level 6 (Advanced) Able to ski in control in a variety of conditions/terrain Refining my skills on steeps, forests, drops off piste riding and freestyle skills I can ski in control in nearly all terrain, also including off piste in a variety of conditions
Level 7 (Expert) I can ride in a variety of snow conditions/terrain at speed. I like to challenge myself and am experienced in powder Expanding my mountain experience to include slack/backcountry riding, tighter trees, drops, freestyle. Include challenging myself at higher speeds I can ski aggressively and am in control while skiing in a variety of off piste conditions.

Preparing for a Ski Trip! Should I rent or buy?

Helmets

Helmets are extremlly importent for safety while skiing and its very encouraged to use one while Skiing. (Its very encouraged but you can choose not to) Proper Ski helmets are quite expensive to purchase and every ski resort will most likely have rentals for helemts. I would recommand you to rent your helemt unless you belive you will invest a lot of time in this sport.

Goggles

Ski goggles are unfortunaely only a buy option as its very uncommon to find places that rent them. They are quite important as they help protect your eyes from the harsh conditions up in the mountain. The quaility of goggles however ranges from low to very high prices so is completly fine to just purchase a cheap one as they still get the job done. (Might be unconfortable) However, if you don't want to buy one you can always just forgo them for some sunglasses instead. (Not the best choice)

Skis and Boots

Skis and boots are the most important gear of a skiing trip (Duh) but they are very very expensives. They can run you up a couple thousand bucks and also counting the maintenance. So unless skiing its a hobby for you just rent it.

Cold Resistence Clothing

Unless you are used to living in negative degrees temperature. You will have to invest in some clothing that helps you not die and get frostbite. You can't rent these usually so I would recommand to just buy them. Usually 3 sets will be good enough for a 5 day trip. Personally, I recommand getting uniqlo Ultra warm heattech clothing. I use them myself and it works really well (Haven't died to the cold yet).

Ski jackets and pants

These pieces of clothing are the final part to your 2/3 layers of cloths you will need to wear (If you aren't just immune to the cold) and are quite important. They are usually water resistence so falling in the snow won't make you wet and ruin all your other cloths. You can purchase these but renting them is also a valid option (Buying costs quite a bit). If you don't ski often, rent. If you do, buy.
